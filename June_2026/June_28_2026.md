### Alibaba's milking of Claude

###### Context about the company

For context, Alibaba was founded in 1999 to connect Chinese manufacturers with overseas buyers (B2B), and over time grew into China's largest e-commerce company. Its holdings now span logistics, cloud services, finance, and media (like Taobao, a consumer marketplace, and AliExpress which is for cross-border shipping).

It IPO'd in 2014, and joined the NYSE. The current leaders, Chairman Joe Tasi and CEO Eddie Wu took over in 2023 with the intention of returning Alibaba to its "startup roots", a strategic U-turn...it thus got split into 6 semi-independent business groups (1+6+N structure). Since then, Wu has recentralized, taking direct control of the Cloud and China e-commerce units and pursuing an aggressive AI-driven strategy.

The company underwent heavy pressure in the 2020s, including a Chinese antitrust crackdown, a record fine, and co-founder Jack Ma who retreated from public life after lots of criticism. The company has also had a sloppy pivot into AI, and its stock hasn't done too well in 2026.

###### Qwen

Alibaba's AI effort centers around Qwen (Chinese name is longer), a family of LLMs developed through Alibaba Cloud. They actually released Qwen 3.5 in early 2026! The company frames its whole (or most of) its mission around AI.

The main thing that distinguishes Qwen from its competitors is that it's released as open-weight models. It now competes domestically with DeepSeek and Tencent (and internationally with Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind models, of course).

###### The Claude distillation accusation

In late June (literally this past week), news broke that Anthropic accused Alibba of extracting Claude's capabilities.

On June 10th, Anthropic sent a letter to the Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs (addressed to Chair Tim Scott and Ranking Member Elizabeth Warren). The letter alleged that Alibaba's Qwen carried out 28.8 million exchanges with Claude using ~ 25,000 fraudulent accounts between April and then, calling it the "largest known distillation attack" on the company to date. Its most commercially valuable skills, too, were targeted: software engineering, agentic reasoning, and long horizon tasks.

"Distillation" is the act of repeatedly prompting a strong model through its API, collecting outputs, and using those outputs as training data to improve a competing model. While this is a standard method in academic AI research (a smaller model learning from a stronger one), it obviously gets murky when the larger model is a competitor AND there was no license granted.

This wasn't Anthropic's only accusation of this type: by Feb 2026, it had already named 3 other Chinese labs (DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax) as having done the same thing. Together, they had about 24,000 fraudulent accounts + 16 million exchanges. None of them came close to Alibaba.

Alibaba, of course, denies these allegations, which haven't yet been verified independently. A tech-law specialist at Tsinghua University (Li Kai) characterized Anthropic's accusations as "industriual regulation by complaint" which is bascially the use of natioanl security framing to achivee a commercial objective that couldn't be won via regular IP litigation. There's also no settled definition of "adversarial distillation" yet, but maybe there will be soon after this?

### OpenAI and Broadcom's new chip

OpenAI and Broadcom announced their partnership in October of 2025 (with the purpose of building 10 gigawatts of custom AI accelerators) but the first time this partnership came to fruition was June 24th, 2026 (4 days ago): the first chip called Japaleno.

Jalapeno is OpenAI's first custom inference processor, co-developed and designed (the whole process) in just 9 months, which might be the fastest ever time for this type of thing. OpenAI used its own models to help with the design. It's been built specifically for inference (running models rather than training them).

This move will help solve OpenAI's problem of complete reliance on Nvidia's GPUs (although training will still probably rely on Nvidia). The initial deployment is set for the end of 2026.

A little background on Broadcom as a company (NASDAQ: AVGO). They're an American semiconductor + software giant led by CEO Hock Tan. Unlike Nvidia, which sells off-the-shelf chips, Broadcom co-designs and custom silicon (ASICs) and networking gear for big tech companies; as you can imagine, they benefitted A LOT from the AI boom.

### An update on Fable 5's ban

The US Commerce Department's export control directive happened on June 12th, 2026. And still, API calls to Fable 5 return errors; there's still no official restoration date.

The closest things to restoration dates are between July 8th and August 1st. On July 8th, Anthropic's updated privacy policy (which requires government-issued ID and biometric verification via Persona, Peter Thiel's identity platform) takes effect. This has been widely understood as a mechanism for restoring fable 5 to verified US citizens without requiring the explort control directive to be fully lifted (so international users would still be resicted to Opus 4.8 at best).

What about August 1st? That's when the 60-day window expired under the June 2nd Executive Order for NSA, Treasury, and CISA to build a classified benchmarking process + voluntary pre-release framework for covered frontier models. This has been understood as Anthropic's best path back to a good standing in the US government's eyes (IF they follow that framework for future model releases).

One more date: June 25th. Reuters and AP confirmed NSA testing (which is what led to the ban under Project Glasswing). An unidentified US official (who had access to the models) told AP that Mythos identified vulnerabilities in hours but didn't necessarily exploit them, which is a deviation from the earlier "breached classified systems" narrative/framing that led to the ban in the first place, and were circulating.

This restoration question, perhaps, has become secondary to the governance question (rightly so?): the export control ban is less a product decision and more fo a preview of what AI regulation looks like when there's no charted process for it. This is important for EVERY AI lab, not just Anthropic!

### Gemini 3.5's quiet delay to July 

According to data partly from Polymarket and partly from insider reports, Google has pushed the availability of Gemini 3.5 Pro from June (original promised time) to July. Why? engineers found issues with token efficieny and long-horizon task performance.

The model was originally announced at Google I/O (May 19th, 2026) to be released in June which drew groans from the audience, who expected it released THAT DAY.

Missing the deadline is a bigger deal/problem for Google than it seems, even if the technical reason behind the delay seems fair, as long-horizon task performance is not something you'd want to ship earlier than it's ready. They're losing credibility.

### Colorado AI Act takes effect June 30th

Again, this is the first comprehensive state AI law in the US to actually go into force. 

The law regulates "high-risk" AI systems used in big decisions affecting things (like employment, education, housing, healthcare, financial services, government services, insurance, and legal services for Colorado residents).

The journey to this point has been a twisty/turbulent one. Look no further than the originally intended date: Feb 1st, 2026. Colorado Governor Jared Polis signed SB 189, which narrowed/amended it a lot, and pushed its effective-date to January 2027.

What kinds of regulations did Polis eliminate? Here's a few: the duty of care for algorithmic discrimination, removing deployer obligations to maintain risk management programs, and dropping certain reporting mandates. There's still, however, a ["transparency-focused framework centered on disclosure requirements when automated decisionmaking tools are used in consequential decisions"](https://unrot.co/blogs/today-top-10-ai-news-june-26-2026)

### Miscellaneous (i.e. questions I had while putting this together, and their respective answers)

###### 1 + 6 + N structure 

This indicates a holding company, 6 major business groups beneath it (Cloud, Taobao/Tmall, local services, logistics digital media, and international commerce) and N for the many smaller ventures/subsidiaries below those.

This structure is one of many. Here are some others: 

- Functional -- grouped by activity (like marketing, engineering, finance). Each division is semi-autonomous. Common in smaller companies
- Divisional -- grouped by product, region, or market (each division is semi-autonomous)
- Holding company/conglomerate -- a parent that owns many distinct businesses that each operate independently (like Alphabet, Berkshire Hathaway)
- Matrix -- each employee reports along 2 axes at once (for example, function and project). This is meant to create flexibility but can get confusing, I imagine
- Flat/flatarchy -- very few management layers, common in startups
- Networks -- a lean core that outsources most functions to outside partners

###### Open vs. closed weight models

- An open-weight model's parameters are published, so anyone can download/run/fine-tune/modify it on their own hardware (like Meta's Llama, Alibaba's Qwen, DeepSeek, Mistral).
- A closed-weight model, of course, lets you access it only through an API or app, not the actual underlying file (like Claude, GPT, Gemini).

Open-weight models can be deeply customized, but closed-weight models are restricted so the only modifications available are set by the provider. Important distinction in national security right now!

###### Models can be leaders in coding and reasoning -- anything else? Or are these two the main categories?

Coding and reasoning are the most flashy and talked-about capabilities since they are the most commercially valuable, but here are other ones:

- Math
- Multimodal (capable of vision, audio, video, etc)
- Long-context (handling huge inputs like entire books, codebases, etc without losing track of stuff)
- Agentic/tool-use (aka "long-horizon" work)
- Writing/creative
- Multilingual
- Safety/alignment (not exactly Grok's speciality)
- Speed/cost-efficiency

###### More on "Industrial Regulation by Complaint" (Anthropic's accusation Alibaba as characterized by AI-law specialist Li Kai)

Beware that this framing is from someone who spoke to the Global Times (a Chinese state-affiliated media) so there's a chance it's not as neutral as it could be.

Here's why, Kai thinks, it would be difficult for Anthropic to achieve their desired national security outcomes WITHOUT this type of thing: a normal IP (intellectual property) lawsuit would require Anthropic to prove infringement under existing law before a judge, which would be really hard because there are no settled laws for prompting public API and training on outputs (in fact, it's a commonly accepted method in academic AI research)

Thus, by routing it through Congress as a security matter, the claim can drive new sanctions ot rules (like the Hagerty-Kim amendment) without ever having to win a legal argument. The Hagerty-Kim amendment is a proposed amendment to the National Defense Authorization Act that would blacklist any Chinese firm found to be improperly accessing American AI model outputs to train competing models.

A broader worry is regulatory capture (a private company effectively shaping government policy in a way that conveniently handicaps a competitor).

###### What are ASICs, the speciality of Broadcom?

ASIC = application-specific integrated circuit

An ASIC is a chip designed to do one job very well, rather than general-purpose work. So it's less flexible than Nvidia's GPUs (general-purpose, many workloads, programmable).

OpenAI's and Broaccom's new cip, Jalapeno, is an ASIC.

Broadcom's business model reminds me of Palantir's: they design tech products tailored to whatever company they have a contract with, rather than making general-purpose products to sell to anyone relevant.

###### Google uses TPUs rather than GPUs -- what's the difference and does that make them NOT rely on Nvidia as much as the other labs do?

TPU = tensor processing unit, Google's custom-designed ASIC, whose specific purpose is to accelerate AI and ML workloads. It's an alternative to GPUs, like the one Nvidia supplies (which makes Google the least-Nvidia-dependent of the major labs). It's been designing/creating TPUs since 2015, and have begun to rent them to external customers via Google Cloud.

That said, Google still buys SOME of Nvidia's GPUs because its cloud customers still want them (many companies build on CUDA and expect Nvidia hardware). CUDA = compute unified device architecture (Nvidia's software platform that lets developed write programs that run on its GPUs, which is unqiue since GPUs were originally built for graphics not general computation).

Google's move here (relative independence/lack of reliance on Nvidia) is something major tech companies are trying to emulate. For example, Amazon's 2 custom AI chips that were build by AWS are Trainium (for training models) and Inferentia (for inference).

###### What is "gigawatt-scale" in datacenters?

A "gigawatt" tells us about how much electric power a datacenter (or cluster of them) draws. It's not about computing capacity directly, but the power is such a binding constraint on AI, so gigawatts also indirectly tell us about compute.

Here's a sense for what a gigawatt can power:

- 1 watt = a small LED bulb
- 1 kilowatt = a microwave or space heater
- 1 megawatt = a small factory (or 800-1,000 average American homes)
- 1 gigawatt = more than the output of a large nuclear reactor (enough to power ~ 750,000 homes, or a mid-sized city)

So when OpenAI and Broadcom talk about deploying 10 gigawatts of custom AI accelerators, that's like TEN NUCLEAR REACTORS' WORTH OF ELECTRICITY dedicated to running chips. The major bottleneck in AI is no longer # of chips (and expenses associated with buying them); it's about finding the power to run them!


###### The Pentagon's 1260H military companies list (and how Alibaba is trying to remove itself from this list)

Section 1260H is a roster of companies the Pentagon judges to be "Chinese military companies" (accused of association with China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology, MIIT). The Pentagon is required by a 2021 law to maintain the list, which now has 188 firms (up from 134 in 2025).

Among these are Baidu, BYD, and Alibaba as of June 8th, 2026. Addition to the list doesn't immediately mean sanctions -- it's more of a signal to investors (the main harm is procurement: no more selling goods/services to the DOD as of June 30th).

Alibaba sued the DOD a week ago, seeking removal, which might work (Xiaomi sued successfully, so precedent has been set).


###### What exactly is an export control directive?

At its core, it's a government order restricting what can be sold/shipped/transferred across borders to countries, companies, and individuals. While the first uses of these things were restriction of physical goods, they're increasingly for tech, software, and technical know-how.

Many governments use them to keep hold of sensitive capabilities (like things of military/national-security value) out of rivals' hands.

Export controls are central to the AI/chip conversation! Their use in AI is a testimony to the fact that they are policy/security tools rather than economic/trade tools. Some examples:

- US restricted Nvidia's advanced GPUs from being sold to China (main case). Nvidia, then, intentionally weakened the chips to slip under performance thresholds which US tightened restrictions to catch, too 
- US restrictions on ASML (Dutch) lithography machines and Applied Materials tools -- purpose was to block China from manufacturing chips
